About Day of the Dead

Día de Muertos (Day of the Dead) is a traditional Mexican holiday celebrated on November 1st and 2nd, where families gather to honor and remember their deceased loved ones.

Ofrenda (Altar)

Families create altars or "ofrendas" in their homes, including the four elements: water, wind, earth, and fire. These altars often have multiple levels representing heaven, earth, and the underworld.

Calaveras and Pan de Muerto

Sugar skulls (calaveras) and bread of the dead (pan de muerto) are traditional items placed on the altar. Sugar skulls often have the name of the deceased inscribed on the forehead.

Cempasúchitl Flowers

Cempasúchitl (marigold) flowers are used to decorate altars and graves. Their pungent odor is believed to attract the spirits of the deceased.

La Catrina

La Catrina, a female skeleton dressed in early 20th-century upper-class style, has become an iconic symbol of the Day of the Dead celebrations.

Celebrations and Parades

Day of the Dead celebrations often include parades featuring representations of La Catrina, skeletons, and other icons of death and the underworld.
